Why Art?

The connection between science and philosophy, and between the art and philosophy, are widely recognized; by contrast, the relationship between science and art tends to be less visible. In the following, we discuss this connection and the benefits of featuring art in the cross-disciplinary magazine (MeSPhA).

Art and science are not separate domains but intertwined ways of knowing that share methods of observation, representation, imagination and public communication. Across history, they have flowed into one another: scientific ideas have inspired artistic forms and techniques, while artistic practices—visualization, narrative, metaphor—have shaped how knowledge is produced, taught, and shared. Supporting the dialogue between them strengthens creativity, public understanding and democratic participation in knowledge.

Historical Highlights

Ancient Greece

  • Pythagoras: Early bridge between mathematics and art through the discovery of numerical ratios in musical harmony, showing how abstract number relations shape aesthetic experience.

Renaissance (14th to the 17th century)

During the Italian Renaissance — a broad cultural movement rooted in humanism that unfolded roughly from the 14th to the 17th century — artistic, scientific, and philosophical pursuits were frequently combined in the same individuals. Below we will focus on a few of these figures.
  • Dante Alighieri: Medieval model of public knowledge; by writing philosophical and natural reflections in the vernacular (Convivio), he advocated accessible dissemination of complex ideas. Dante's "Divina Commedia" is not only a literary work but also a philosophical and scientific text that reflects the cultural and intellectual climate of its era.
  • Leonardo da Vinci: Emblematic fusion of artistic mastery and empirical investigation—anatomical drawing, engineering sketches and proportional studies (e.g., the Vitruvian figure) illustrate how visual practice can be a scientific method.
  • Galileo Galilei: is widely regarded as a father of modern science for his transformative contributions to physics, astronomy, and the development of the scientific method. Less often noted is his humanistic and artistic background. Influenced by his father, Vincenzo Galilei — a prominent musician and music theorist who challenged Pythagorean tuning — Galileo trained in music and investigated sound and vibration. He was also a clear and influential prose writer (for example, The Assayer and the Dialogues), and his practical skills in optics and instrument‑making led to important advances in telescopes and observational techniques. This combination of excellence in science and art informed his experimental design and observational practice, helping to reshape how we perceive and study the cosmos.

Modern Period

  • Pablo Picasso’s early 20th‑century break with traditional form and perspective—most famously expressed in Cubism—was shaped by a constellation of influences: Cézanne’s reinterpretation of form, African and Iberian art, advances in visual technologies (photography, X‑ray imagery) and optics, and broader intellectual currents of the time (including interest in modern psychology). These scientific, technological, and cultural developments helped create new conceptual frameworks for representing space, simultaneity and multiple viewpoints, enabling Picasso and his collaborators (notably Georges Braque) to reinvent pictorial space.
  • Werner Heisenberg: Quantum mechanics introduced fundamental indeterminacy into physics, reshaping philosophical and cultural understandings of uncertainty—an influence felt across artistic movements that embraced complexity and ambiguity.
  • Primo Levi: he was both a professional chemist and a writer. He aimed to bridge the gap between the imaginative world of literature and the analytical world of science, believing that such a gap was absurd.
  • Charles Percy Snow: he highlights the importance of cross-disciplinary literacy. Encouraging scientists to engage with the humanities and vice versa can foster innovation, improve problem-solving, and create a more cohesive intellectual society. His thesis remains a reference point for debates on education, research, and cultural understanding.

Added Value of Linking Science with Art

  • Enhances communication: Artistic techniques (visualization, storytelling, metaphor) make complex scientific ideas accessible and memorable to diverse publics.
  • Stimulates creativity and discovery: Cross-disciplinary practices generate novel questions, methods and prototypes that neither field would produce alone.
  • Improves education and literacy: Integrating art into science teaching fosters deeper understanding, critical thinking, and motivation across learning styles.
  • Broadens societal engagement: Culturally resonant expressions of science invite wider participation in public debates (e.g., climate, health), strengthening democratic decision-making.
  • Expands technological and cultural innovation: Artistic experimentation often anticipates new uses for technologies and reveals social implications early in their development.
  • Counters reductionism: Artistic perspectives help preserve complexity, context and values that purely reductive approaches can overlook.
